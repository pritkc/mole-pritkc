Jacobian

Author: Miguel A. Dumett
Abstract: This document presents mimetic differences gradient and divergence operators in structured curvilinear geometries. It uses mimetic interpolation operators to move quantities among the staggered grids of the mesh.

Introduction

Suppose a PDE is given on a physical spatial domain \(\mathcal P\) in three-dimensions (3D), with coordinates \(x,y,z\). Suppose \(\mathcal P\) is the result of a bijective smooth map \(\mathcal X\) given by

\[\begin{split}\begin{aligned} x & = & x(\xi,\eta,\kappa) \\ y & = & y(\xi,\eta,\kappa) \\ z & = & z(\xi,\eta,\kappa), \end{aligned}\end{split}\]

and that the inverse map of \(\mathcal X\) is \(\Theta\) which is given by

\[\begin{split}\begin{aligned} \xi & = & \xi(x,y,z) \\ \eta & = & \eta(x,y,z) \\ \kappa & = & \kappa(x,y,z), \end{aligned}\end{split}\]

and it maps \(\mathcal P\) onto a 3D logical Cartesian domain \(\mathcal L\). If one defines a staggered grid on \(\mathcal L\), composed of faces \(F\) and centers/boundaries \(C\), then \(\mathcal X(C \cup F)\) is an structured grid on \(\mathcal P\), with centers/boundaries \(\mathcal C = \mathcal X(C)\) and faces \(\mathcal F = \mathcal X(F)\).

The Jacobian of the transformation \(\mathcal X\) is given by

\[\begin{split}J = \frac{\partial(x,y,z)}{\partial(\xi,\eta,\kappa)} = \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} x_\xi & x_\eta & x_\kappa \\ y_\xi & y_\eta & y_\kappa \\ z_\xi & z_\eta & z_\kappa \end{array} \right].\end{split}\]

For \(u:\mathcal X \to \mathbb R\), with \(u = u(x,y,z) = u(x(\xi,\eta,\kappa),y(\xi,\eta,\kappa),z(\xi,\eta,\kappa))\) and hence \(u = u(\xi,\theta,\kappa)\), the chain rule implies

\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} u_\xi &= u_x x_\xi + u_y y_\xi + u_z z_\xi \\ u_\eta &= u_x x_\eta + u_y y_\xi + u_z z_\eta \\ u_\kappa &= u_x x_\kappa + u_y y_\xi + u_z z_\kappa \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

or equivalently,

\[\begin{split}\begin{aligned} \left[ \begin{array}{c} u_\xi \\ u_\eta \\ u_\kappa \end{array} \right] = \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} x_\xi & y_\xi & z_\xi \\ x_\eta & y_\eta & z_\eta \\ x_\kappa & y_\kappa & z_\kappa \end{array} \right] \left[ \begin{array}{c} u_x \\ u_y \\ u_z \end{array} \right] = J^T \left[ \begin{array}{c} u_x \\ u_y \\ u_z \end{array} \right] \end{aligned}\end{split}\]

Hence

\[\begin{split}\left[ \begin{array}{c} u_x \\ u_y \\ u_z \end{array} \right] = (J^T)^{-1} \left[ \begin{array}{c} u_\xi \\ u_\eta \\ u_\kappa\end{array} \right].\end{split}\]

Since

\[\begin{split}\left[ \begin{array}{ccc} a & b & c \\ d & e & f \\ g & h & i \end{array} \right]^{-1} = \frac{1}{\Delta} \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} ei - fh & ch-bi & bf - ce \\ fg - di & ai - cg & cd - af \\ dh - eg & bg - ah & ac - bd \end{array} \right],\end{split}\]

where

\(\Delta = a(ei-fh) - b(di-fg) + c(dh-eg)\).

If one denotes

\[\begin{split} J^T = \begin{pmatrix} (1) = x_\xi & (2) = y_\xi & (3) = z_\xi \\ (4) = x_\eta & (5) = y_\eta & (6) = z_\eta \\ (7) = x_\kappa & (8) = y_\kappa & (9) = z_\kappa \end{pmatrix} \end{split}\]

then

\[\begin{split} (J^T)^{-1} = \frac{1}{\Delta} \begin{pmatrix} (5)(9) - (6)(8) & (3)(8) - (2)(9) & (2)(6) - (3)(5) \\ (6)(7) - (4)(9) & (1)(9) - (3)(7) & (3)(4) - (1)(6) \\ (4)(8) - (5)(7) & (2)(7) - (1)(8) & (1)(5) - (2)(4) \end{pmatrix} \end{split}\]

with

\(\Delta = (1) ((5)(9) - (6)(8)) - (2)((4)(9) - (6)(7)) + (3)((4)(8) - (5)(7)).\)

If one uses the gradient to approximate the partial derivatives of the Jacobian, then

\[ J_G^T = I_{xyz}^{F \to C} {\tilde G}_{\xi \eta \kappa} \]

where \({\tilde G}_{xyz}\) is the same as \(G_{xyz}\) with \({\hat I}_p\) replaced by \(I_{p+2}\), the identity matrix of order \(p+2\). If one computes the Jacobian at the centers then the physical gradient is given by

\[ G_{xyz} = I_{xyz}^{C \to F} (J_G^T)^{-1} I_{\xi \eta \kappa}^{F \to C} G_{\xi \eta \kappa}. \]

Similarly, one can construct the Jacobian based on the divergence operator.
